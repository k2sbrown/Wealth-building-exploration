Asset Poverty

Measuring what people own—not just what they earn—reveals a distinct and often hidden dimension of economic insecurity.

Key scholars: Haveman & Wolff  ·  Gibson-Davis  ·  Sherraden  ·  Oliver & Shapiro

Income poverty measures what flows into a household each month. Asset poverty measures what a household can fall back on when that flow stops. The two concepts overlap but frequently diverge—and ignoring assets leaves a significant share of economic vulnerability unmeasured.

Core definition

A household is asset poor if the wealth it holds is insufficient to sustain basic needs at the poverty level for a defined period—typically three months—in the absence of any income.

This framework has been developed and refined across a substantial research literature (Haveman & Wolff, 2004; Oliver & Shapiro, 1995). Without an adequate asset buffer, even a temporary income shock—job loss, illness, an unexpected bill—can produce lasting harm that income statistics never capture.

How it is measured

The dominant fill-the-gap approach asks whether a household's net worth could sustain it at the poverty line for the threshold period. Three variants are common: net worth (all assets minus debts, including home equity); liquid assets (readily accessible financial assets only); and net worth excluding home equity, which strips out the illiquidity of owner-occupied housing. Each produces a different rate and a different picture of vulnerability. More recently, researchers have disaggregated net worth poverty into asset poverty (too few assets) and debt poverty (debts that overwhelm adequate assets)—a distinction that matters for both policy design and outcomes.

Relationship to income poverty measures

The Official Poverty Measure (OPM) and the Supplemental Poverty Measure (SPM) both define poverty through the lens of income and consumption: the OPM compares pre-tax cash income to a threshold; the SPM refines this by adding noncash benefits and subtracting necessary expenses like taxes and medical costs. Neither incorporates wealth. Asset poverty is conceptually complementary to both—it uses the same poverty-line thresholds as its reference point, but asks about the stock of resources rather than the flow.

The overlap is real: households that are both income poor and asset poor face the deepest hardship, and Haveman and Wolff identify this group explicitly through a joint income/asset poverty measure. But the divergence matters equally. Many households above the OPM or SPM threshold hold little or no net worth—income-stable but financially fragile. Conversely, some income-poor households, particularly older homeowners, hold meaningful assets. Asset poverty captures a dimension of insecurity that income measures, however refined, are not designed to see.

"Asset poverty more than doubled the risk of food insecurity relative to debt poverty."
— Gibson-Davis, Keister & Gennetian (2023)

What the research shows

Haveman and Wolff's work established several durable findings: asset poverty is widespread even among households above the income poverty line; it is substantially higher for minority households, particularly Black and Hispanic families; and it does not move in lockstep with the business cycle—periods of income growth do not automatically reduce asset gaps. The takeaway is that asset poverty has its own dynamics, shaped by structural barriers to wealth accumulation that income-focused policy leaves largely untouched.

Policy responses

Recognition that income transfers alone cannot close the asset gap has driven interest in asset-building approaches. The intellectual foundation is Sherraden's Assets and the Poor (1991), which argued that institutions—not individual behavior—determine whether low-income households accumulate wealth. Child Development Accounts (CDAs), seeded at birth and built over time, are the leading policy vehicle; experimental evidence from the SEED for Oklahoma Kids program shows effects on savings, parental expectations, and maternal mental health.
